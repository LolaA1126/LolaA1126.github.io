Optimization and Finite Element Analysis of Wing Design

Figure 1: Initial wing gometry modeled in Spaceclaim
This project involved designing and optimizing a 15 meter long aircraft wing made from 2024-T36 Aluminum to minimize weight using ANSYS. The geometry model contains the skin, spars, and ribs of the wing. Analysis was conducted assuming typical loading conditions in flight, that is, fixed to the fuselage and experiencing loads due to the weight of the structure as well as aerodynamic pressure. The aerodynamic forces were simulated using a negative pressure on the top skin in ANSYS and a positive pressure on the bottom wing skin surface. These constraints were dictated by the assignment guidelines and roughly estimate the way a wing generates lift.

The design variables included the thicknesses of the major structural elements, as well as number, location, and shapes of spars and ribs. A minimum safety factor of 1.5 and maximum a tip deflection of 0.375 m are also imposed.

Approach and Outcome

The first step in this project was to simulate the initial design given to us with all surfaces being 10 cm thick. Each group memeber then split up design variables to explore. I looked into the impact of a nonstandard spar configuration, as well as the effect of changing the number and location of the ribs. I also explored some designs which included cut outs in the ribs and spars. We then looked at all of our results and recommend a design based on our exploration of these variables. Our initial baseline design had a mass of 4840 kg and experienced a displacement of 0.683 m with a factor of safety of 1.29. Our final design has a mass of 8050.4 kg, a displacement of 0.37156 m with a factor of safety of 1.81. We concluded that putting holes into the spars to minimize their role as major structural components was the best approach.

Four Rib Design

Figure 6: Equivalent Stress of four rib design
Table 2: Parameters of four rib geometry
For this model, I increased the number of ribs to four. This allowed us to isolate the impact of changing the number of ribs from the number in the default model. I ran OptiSlang in ANSYS to determine the optimal thicknesses for all of the surfaces that result in the lowest acceptable factor of safety and the highest acceptable value of deformation. This design iteration showed that increasing the number of ribs does not change the tip deflection significantly, and only ends up increasing the mass of the final design. By increasing the number of ribs, you might increase the number of locations where load is transferred to the spars in practice, which should lower the total bending moment on the spar, but in this model increasing the number ribs does not affect the load of the spar significantly, because the aerodynamic load is transferred to the spar by the skin itself. As a result of this analysis, I concluded that we should stick with the original number of ribs, or even decrease down to one rib.

Shorter Aft Spar Design

For this model, I decreased the length of the aft spar to about 7.5 meters long. I theorized that this would be an effective way to reduce weight since the highest stresses are located at the base of the wing. I then ran OptiSlang to determine the optimal thicknesses for all of the surfaces. The parameters for the lightest design which passed all of our success criteria are shown in table 3. Based on this simulation, I recommended that the final design includes some version of shortened spars as this is an effective way to reduce weight while meeting success criteria.
