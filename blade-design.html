DWind Turbine Blade Design, Optimization, and Testing

Figure 1: 3D Printed Blade inside of wind turbine testing setup
This was a group project for a fluid dynamics lab course. We were tasked with designing a wind turbine blade optimized for maximum power production at a specific target wind speed.

Given that the wind experienced by the turbine would not be constant in a natural environment, we assumed that velocity profile would vary in speed according to a Weibull distribution ranging from 0 m/s to 8 m/s. To maximize total average power distribution, we identified the speed that was both most likely to occur and capable of producing the highest theoretical power for a given turbine size. To do this, we multiplyied the theoretical power output, given by equation 1, by the probability of each wind speed occurring. This analysis indicated that the optimal design wind speed was 5.4 m/s.

We then performed finite element analysis (FEA) on the initial blade geometry to evaluate yield stresses and deflection magnitudes at the blade tip. Two loading scenarios were analyzed: one under optimal design conditions and another under maximum possible operating conditions. Our analysis revealed that the initial blade design experienced significant deflection at the tip, even under optimal conditions. To address this, we scaled the chord length using a function that increased chord more significantly toward the tip than at the base, resulting in a fatter, more rectangular blade shape. For wind tunnel testing, we selected a low wind speed and applied a braking torque to achieve a rotation rate as close to 2000 RPM as possible. We then incrementally increased the braking torque until the blade stalled. Once stall occurred, we incremented the wind speed in steps of approximately 1 m/s, repeating this process as many times as the allotted testing time permitted. Finally, we compared our results to our design conditions by plotting power output versus RPM and power output versus wind speed. While our blade performed optimally under the designed conditions, some technical limitations encountered during the lab testing prevented us from conclusively verifying this outcome. These limitations are explored in greater detail in this report.

Analytical Modeling

To simplify the dynamics, the system's motors and camera were modeled as point masses, with the gimbal arms assumed to have negligible mass. Using three non-inertial reference frames (aligned with gimbal arms and the camera lens), dynamic analyses were performed using both the Euler-Lagrange and Differential-Algebraic Equation (DAE) methods. Key steps included:
  • Deriving equations of motion using rotation matrices and potential/kinetic energy calculations.
  • Solving these equations numerically in MATLAB, employing Jacobian operators and the ode45 solver.

Findings

  • Balanced vs. Unbalanced Systems: When the CG aligns with the intersection of all axes (balanced), the camera experiences lower forces but higher vibrations. These vibrations, attributed to modeling approximations (e.g., motors and camera as point masses), suggest the need for improved modeling with moments of inertia.
  • Method Performance: The Euler-Lagrange approach was computationally efficient but less flexible for prescribed changes, while the DAE method supported more complex conditions at the cost of higher computational time.
